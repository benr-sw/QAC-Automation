## Teacher Background Knowledge:

- Ancient Athens is known for its invention of democracy, which emerged after reformers challenged the rule of wealthy nobles. Cleisthenes established a council and an Assembly, giving ordinary citizens a voice. Athenian democracy flourished under Pericles, who furthered citizen participation by introducing pay for public service. Despite Athens's achievements during the golden age, Plato criticized democracy, advocating for philosopher-kings. Nevertheless, Athenian democracy's influence endures, with its principles inspiring modern democratic systems worldwide.

- The Greco-Persian Wars were fought from about 492 B.C.E. to 449 B.C.E. between the small city-states of Greece and the First Persian Empire, which was one of the largest empires of all time. The wars ended with the Greeks maintaining their independence from Persia. Greeks remember the two most famous battles of the Persian Wars as evidence of their people's great courage and resourcefulness. At the Battle of Marathon in 490 B.C.E., an army from the city of Athens expelled the Persians from the Greek mainland. At the Battle of Salamis in 480 B.C.E., the superior Greek navy lured Persian ships into a strait, trapping them and ultimately destroying the Persian navy. This battle ended any hope of Persian forces taking mainland Greece.

- From 460 to 446 B.C.E. and then 431 to 404 B.C.E., Athens and Sparta fought against each other in what is known as the Peloponnesian Wars. These two rival city-states fought for dominance of the southern part of the Greek peninsula, which is known as the Peloponnese. Sparta won the war, but the long conflict weakened both cities. Other parts of Greece soon came to dominate the region. First Thebes and then Macedon rose to power.

## Learning Objectives:

- Students will be able to analyze the causes and consequences of the Persian and Peloponnesian Wars.
- Students will be able to explain how Athens and Sparta sometimes cooperated to defend Greece and other times fought each other for power.
- Students will be able to evaluate how these wars shaped the strength and eventual decline of the Greek city-states.

## Student Outcomes:

- I can describe why the Persian and Peloponnesian Wars happened.
- I can explain how Athens and Sparta worked together in one war but became enemies in another.
- I can tell what happened to Greece as a result of these wars.

## Article 1: The Persian Wars (taught with "Coming Together, Falling Apart") (Pacing: 45 minutes)

### Lesson Plan:

1. Begin the lesson by reviewing the essential question. Have students conduct a picture walk in their student editions.

2. Have students read the intro article "Coming Together, Falling Apart."
   - a. After reading, students should write their responses to the questions at the end of the article and discuss their answers in pairs.
   - b. Ask the whole class to make a prediction about how this article might relate to the part of Greek history they will study this week. ***Accept all reasonable answers.***

3. Show students the video "Persian Leftovers," from related media, as an introduction to who the Persian people were and who some of their leaders were. This video talks about two different examples of writing.
   - a. What primary source tells us about Cyrus the Great? ***the Cyrus Cylinder***
   - b. What primary source tells us about the leader Darius the Great? ***the Behistun Inscription, carved on the face of a cliff***
   - c. Why are these sources important? ***They tell us what life was like and how these men ruled the empire.***

4. Show students the video "The Persian Empire," from related media. Have students listen for the ways that the empire grew. Ask:
   - a. Tax money was used to create roads to connect the cities. What would the effect of these roads be? ***trade, sharing cultures, movement between cities***
   - b. A single form of money was used for the whole empire. What would have been the effect of everyone using the same money? ***more trade***

5. Read through the first paragraph of the article "The Persian Wars" as a class. Use the image "Map of Ancient Persia," from related media, to have students observe the map of the Persian Empire.
   - a. Discuss: What ancient civilizations that we've learned about this year were now under the rule of the Persians? ***Mesopotamia, Sumer, Babylonia, ancient Egypt, ancient Israel, the Indus River Valley, Phoenicia, Assyria***

6. Provide each student with a copy of the printable Causes and Consequences of Conflicts in Ancient Greece.
   - a. Students will only use the first page of the printable in this lesson. You could choose to wait to provide the second page during the next lesson.

7. Explain to students that as they read the articles and watch the videos, they should be adding information about each event to the printable. They should summarize the event, then add the cause(s) and the consequence(s) of the event as well.

8. Have students read the rest of the article.
   - a. Be sure students add information to their printables.

9. Show students the video "The Persian Wars," from related media.
   - a. Be sure students add any new information to their printables.

10. Display the images "Battle of Marathon," "Battle of Thermopylae," and "Battle of Salamis," from related media. Have students identify the movements of the Persian army and Greek armies in each battle. Summarize each of the battles and its consequences.
    - a. Be sure students add any new information to their printables.

11. Place students in small groups or pairs to read the passages "The First Marathon Runner" and "Holding the Hot Gates." Have students discuss why these battles might have been especially important.
    - a. Then, ask students to discuss and write responses to the "Let's Write" prompt: How did geography affect the outcomes of the Persian Wars? ***Accept all reasonable answers. Possible answer: The Greeks were able to use their knowledge of the challenging geography to their advantage.***

12. As a class, discuss what caused the Persian Wars and the consequences of the wars. ***Answers may vary but could include that the Persian Wars are important to world history. People's lives might be different if the Persians had won the wars. Greek ideas like democracy and trial by jury would not have been passed down to us. With victory over the Persians, the Greeks gained power and started building an empire of their own.***

13. Have students add the Causes and Consequences of Conflicts in Ancient Greece printable to their interactive notebooks.

## Article 2: The Political Leagues (taught with "The Peloponnesian War" and "Primary Source Analysis: Pericles's Funeral Oration") (Pacing: 60 minutes)

### Lesson Plan:

1. Have students retrieve the Causes and Consequences of Conflict in Ancient Greece printable from their interactive notebooks.
   - a. Provide students with the second page of the printable if you did not in the previous lesson.

2. As a class, discuss the following questions:
   - a. What were the two sides in the Persian Wars? ***the Persian Empire and the allied Greek city-states***
   - b. Would you describe the Persian Wars as an internal or external conflict? ***It was an external conflict. The Persian Empire was an external empire, as it was not part of ancient Greece.***
     - i. If necessary, define "external" and "internal."
   - c. What internal conflicts do you predict might occur in ancient Greece? ***Accept all reasonable responses.***

3. Have the students read the article "Political Leagues."
   - a. Ensure that students are completing the second page of the Causes and Consequences of Conflicts in Ancient Greece printable as they read.
   - b. **Differentiation:** Read the article out loud, and model highlighting and underlining reasons for the split between political leagues.

4. Introduce the primary source activity "Perspectives From the Past" by telling students that they will use quotes collected by an ancient historian to explain how the cooperation between Greek city-states began to fall apart.
   - a. Students should work in pairs to read and paraphrase each quote. Then, students will independently write about the perspectives and motivations of the Delian and Peloponnesian Leagues.

5. Before reading "The Peloponnesian War," ask students to share what they believe caused the war to begin. ***Conflict within the Delian League about the behavior of Athens eventually grew into a war between two groups of city-states.***

6. Have students read "The Peloponnesian War" in pairs or small groups.
   - a. Ensure that students are completing the second page of the Causes and Consequences of Conflicts in Ancient Greece printable as they read.

7. Show students the video "The Peloponnesian War 431-404 B.C.E.," from related media.
   - a. Be sure students add any new information to their printables.

8. Have each pair or small group get with another group or pair and compare printables. Have students add any missing information or corrections as they share.
   - a. Have students add the second page of the printable to their interactive notebooks if they have not done so already.
   - b. **Extension activity:** Have students research battles of the Peloponnesian Wars and strategies used by each side. Have students create a poster showing the strengths and weaknesses of each side during the war.

9. Conduct a class picture walk of the image "Pericles' Funeral Oration," found in related media. Have students answer these analysis questions in pairs or small groups:
   - a. What is happening in the painting?
   - b. What do you notice?
   - c. What do you think the title means?

10. Look at the article "Primary Source Analysis: Pericles's Funeral Oration" as a class. Read the first paragraph as a class.

11. Then, read the rest of the article "Primary Source: Pericles's Funeral Oration," one paragraph at a time, as a class. Stop after each paragraph to discuss the analysis questions. Review students' responses as a class.
    - a. Ask:
      - i. What is the main idea of the text?
      - ii. Rewrite the passage in your own words.
    - b. As students read the excerpts, stop and clarify any words they do not understand.
    - c. Encourage students to read the excerpt multiple times for clarity.
    - d. **Differentiation:** Lead students through finding the main idea, and rewrite the first excerpt in your own words before having students do it themselves for the second excerpt.

12. Students will use the article "Primary Source Analysis: Pericles's Funeral Oration" to write a claim and provide evidence and an explanation to support their claim.
