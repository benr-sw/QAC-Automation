## INTRO: Coming Together, Falling Apart

The student council at your school starts the year with a clear purpose: making the school better for everyone. Representatives from each grade sit in a circle, share ideas, and take careful notes. They agree to fix the worst problems first, regardless of which hallway or classroom they are representing. Early meetings feel hopeful. One week, the council members vote to obtain more library books; another week, they approve painting the most worn-out lockers. When the council members disagree, they listen to each other and adjust their plans. Students begin to trust that the council is a fair, united group.

As the year goes on, however, one representative from the oldest grade begins to change their attitude. They argue that since the students in their grade are doing the most fundraising and are the oldest, their classrooms deserve the biggest projects. They take the seat at the head of the table, collect all the suggestion forms, and start to decide which ideas will even get voted on. When committees are formed, they put themselves in charge and choose mostly students from their own grade to help. Slowly, more of the budget and attention flows toward their classrooms. Representatives from younger grades notice that their requests are delayed or ignored. They begin to feel pushed aside and mistrustful. The council that once felt like everyone was working together in one shared effort now feels like a struggle between competing sides.

## The Persian Wars

### The Persian Empire

The Persian Empire existed for more than 200 years. It lasted from around 550 to 330 B.C.E. The empire stretched geographically from Greece to India. The Persians came from a region in southwest Asia that is now called Iran. During this time, this region was called Parsa. The Persians were skilled warriors. They had powerful rulers. Though short-lived compared to some, their empire became one of the largest and most powerful in ancient history.

Persian kings such as Cyrus the Great and Darius I conquered older civilizations, including Mesopotamia and Egypt. These kings claimed lands along the eastern Mediterranean coast. They ruled this huge area by dividing it into provinces. They put governors in charge. The governors collected taxes and kept order for the king. A network of roads connected the cities of the empire. These roads helped soldiers, messengers, and traders move more quickly across long distances. People in the empire spoke many different languages and followed different traditions. However, they were all subjects of the Persian king. For nearly two centuries, Persia was the strongest empire in this part of the world.

### Problems with Persia

While the Persian Empire grew and expanded in the east, the powerful city-states of Athens and Sparta emerged as dominant forces on mainland Greece. Before the Persians tried to conquer mainland Greece, they had already taken control of many Greek city-states along the coast of Asia Minor, in a region called Ionia. Many people in these Ionian cities disliked being ruled by leaders who were loyal to Persia, so they rebelled. Some mainland Greek city-states, including Athens, helped the rebels. The revolt was crushed, but the Persian king was angry. He wanted to punish the Greeks who had supported the rebellion. Around 492 B.C.E., the Persian Empire threatened to conquer Greece. This worried both Athenian and Spartan leaders. Both city-states knew a united front was vital against this common enemy.

Athens had a strong navy. Sparta had a well-trained army. These city-states formed an alliance to repel the Persians. Through a series of battles, the Greeks held off the Persian army. By the end of the Persian Wars, the Greeks had stopped the Persian invasions and kept their independence.

### The Battle of Marathon (490 B.C.E.)

On the plains of Marathon, Athenian citizen soldiers, or **hoplites**, faced a large Persian army. Hoplites were heavily armed with shields and spears. Despite being outnumbered, the Athenians used military strategies and a surprise charge to defeat the Persian forces. The Persian army was stunned that they could be defeated by the Athenians. This defeat sent a shock wave through the Persian empire. The victory brought Greek soldiers together and made them feel more confident in their ability to win the war when the city-states were unified.

### The Battle of Salamis (480 B.C.E.)

The Greek forces had a navy with experienced crews of sailors. The Greek navy was superior to the Persian naval forces. Knowing this, the Greeks strategically lured the Persian navy into the straits of Salamis. A **strait** is a narrow body of water that connects two larger bodies of water. Because of their size, Persian ships could not easily turn around in the straits. This opened up the Persian ships to attack. Greek warships rammed and sank many Persian vessels. This naval battle halted the Persian invasion of Greece. After losing so many ships, the Persian king could no longer easily supply his large army in Greece, so many Persian forces withdrew.

> *[Image: "The Battle of Salamis by Wilhelm von Kaulbach, 1868" — A large dramatic painting depicting the naval battle with ships and warriors.]*

### The Battle of Plataea (479 B.C.E.)

After the Greek naval victory at Salamis, the Persian king Xerxes returned to Asia. He left a large army to finish the conquest of Greece. A united Greek force, led on land by Sparta and joined by Athens and many other city-states, marched to face this army near the town of Plataea. Many of Persia's soldiers were conscripts, or people who had been forced to fight against their wishes. When the fighting began, the heavily armored Greek hoplites broke through the Persian front line. Most of the Persian army, including the general, Mardonius, were captured or killed. At the same time, Greek naval forces launched an attack on Persian ships on the coast. These coordinated attacks destroyed Persia's army and navy. This ended any attempt to invade Greece again.

The Persian Wars had a lasting impact on Greek society. Fighting side by side helped many Greeks see themselves as part of one larger people, not just citizens of separate cities. The soldiers also saw how this unity strengthened them when they worked together. After the Persian Wars, Athens's leadership of the navy gave it greater influence among the Greek city-states. Athens started a new alliance, and many members sent ships or money that helped Athens build a stronger navy and impressive public buildings. This rising power made Sparta and its allies afraid that Athens would dominate Greece. This rivalry threatened the unity that Greek city-states had fought so hard to build.

## Holding the Hot Gates

Thermopylae was a narrow mountain pass in northern Greece. The name "Thermopylae" means "hot gates." It earned this name because of the warm springs that flowed nearby. On one side of the pass rose steep, rocky slopes. On the other side lay the sea. The space between them was so tight that only a small number of soldiers could stand side by side.

During the Persian Wars, a small Greek force, including Spartan soldiers led by King Leonidas, chose Thermopylae as the place to block a much larger Persian army. The Persians could not bring all their troops to the front at once, so Greek soldiers wielding shields and spears held them back. Eventually, a local guide showed the Persians a hidden path through the hills, allowing them to surround the defenders. The Greeks at Thermopylae were defeated, but their stand at the "hot gates" bought time for other city-states to prepare. This event became one of the most famous stories of bravery in Greek history.

## The First Marathon Runner

Our modern marathons trace their history back to the legendary actions of a Greek messenger. A few versions of the legend exist, and historians are unsure which is most accurate. In the stories, a runner had to race from the battlefield to Athens to deliver a critical message about the Battle of Marathon. He may have been asking for reinforcements or declaring victory. The messenger ran 26 miles and 385 yards through the hills of Greece to reach Athens. In each of the versions, after arriving in Athens and delivering the message, the runner collapsed from exhaustion.

Centuries later, people in the modern world were inspired by these stories. When the modern Olympic Games began in 1896, organizers created a long-distance race to honor the legendary run from Marathon to Athens. Today, people around the world compete in races called marathons that are exactly 26.2 miles long.

## The Political Leagues

### The Delian League

After the Persian Wars, many of the Greek city-states realized the need for a unified defense against future threats. In 478 B.C.E., the city-states formed the Delian League. The league was a military agreement led by Athens. The main goal was to protect Greek cities from any future Persian attack, and the members agreed to share their resources. At first, each member city-state either sent warships and soldiers or paid money into the league's **treasury** to keep the alliance strong. They did this every year. The league's treasury was initially located on the island of Delos. The Delian League contributed to a period of cultural prosperity. This was known as the Golden Age of Athens.

The Delian League successfully freed Greek cities in Asia Minor and the Aegean Sea from Persian control. However, Athens began to exercise increasing control over the league. Some city-states began to question why they still had to send so much money to the treasury. Then the treasury was moved to Athens. The money began to be spent on monuments and temples in Athens. Other city-states in Greece grew resentful.

The island city-state of Thasos tried to leave the Delian League. Athens sent its navy to attack the city. The navy soldiers tore down Thasos's walls. They took control of its ships and nearby mines. They forced Thasos to rejoin the league and keep paying money. Because Athens seemed to be ruling the other members instead of treating them as equal partners, many Greeks began to say that the Delian League had turned into an Athenian empire.

### The Peloponnesian League

Sparta and Corinth felt threatened by the Delian League's control over the region. They were not part of the Delian League. They saw it as a threat to their independence. To balance the power, Sparta began reorganizing its alliances with other city-states, including Corinth. The Peloponnesian League began to offer protection to the city-states that did not want to be controlled by Athens, to entice them to ally with Sparta. Sparta and its allies claimed that their league would protect the independence of Greek city-states that did not want to be controlled by Athens. Many city-states that were angry about Athenian power or feared losing their freedom chose to side with Sparta.

As more city-states chose sides between Athens and Sparta, the Greek world split into two rival camps. This deep division contributed to the Peloponnesian War.

## Primary Source Activity: Perspectives from the Past

Thucydides was an Athenian general and historian who lived during the Peloponnesian War. After being exiled from Athens, he wrote a detailed history of the conflict between Athens and Sparta. In his book, he includes speeches that he says are based on what leaders actually said or what best expressed their ideas. These speeches help us understand how Athenians and their allies explained their empire and how Spartans and their allies explained their fears.

## The Peloponnesian War

The rivalry between the Peloponnesian League and the Delian League led to a long and disastrous conflict known as the Peloponnesian War. Sparta was the head of the Peloponnesian League. The Delian League was led by Athens. The war occurred in two stages. The first stage lasted from 460 to 446 B.C.E. The second stage lasted from 431 to 404 B.C.E. It included many of the Greek city-states.

For nearly 50 years, these former allies fought a brutal war. The conflict divided the region of Greece. At first, the Athenian navy dominated in battles at sea. The Athenians controlled trade routes and limited supply lines. Later in the war, Sparta received money from the Persian Empire to build a stronger navy. With this help, the Spartans were able to challenge Athens at sea. They began to win important battles. The Spartan fleet destroyed most of the Athenian ships. Without its navy, Athens could not bring in enough food or supplies and was forced to surrender.

Sparta's strong army also eventually wore down the Athenians. Sparta's allies and battle strategies helped them win key land battles. This cut off Athenian supplies and weakened their grip on power. As the war dragged on, thousands of people crowded inside Athens's city walls to stay safe from Spartan attacks, and a deadly plague swept through the city, killing many people, including Athens's leader, Pericles. Sparta emerged victorious.

The Peloponnesian War left the city-states in Greece weakened and fractured. Athens's former power and influence were shattered. The city's walls were torn down, most of its warships were taken away, and Athens had to give up control of its empire. For a short time, a small group of pro-Spartan leaders called the 30 Tyrants ruled Athens harshly, punished their opponents, and caused fear and unrest in the city.

After the Peloponnesian War, Sparta tried to use its victory to control other Greek city-states. Spartan leaders placed friendly governments in many cities and interfered in their politics. Instead of creating peace, this angered other Greeks and led to even more wars and rebellions. Over time, both Athens and Sparta lost strength as they fought against new enemies. The city-states of Greece became poorer, more divided, and less able to work together. The wars left them weakened and vulnerable at a time when other empires were beginning to grow.

## Primary Source Analysis: Pericles's Funeral Oration

In 431 B.C.E., Pericles gave a funeral **oration**, or a formal speech, for all of the soldiers who had died in the Peloponnesian War. His speech focused on Athenian democracy and achievements. Historians believe the speech was recorded and rewritten by Thucydides, a Greek historian. Since this speech was written in ancient Greek, there are many different translations. This text is an excerpt from a translation of Pericles's speech.

> *[Image: "Pericles Gives the Funeral Speech by Philipp von Foltz, 1852" — A painting depicting Pericles standing and delivering his speech to a large crowd of Athenian citizens, with classical architecture in the background.]*

### Excerpt from Pericles's Funeral Oration

> *I shall begin with our ancestors: it is both just and proper that they should have the honour of the first mention on an occasion like the present. They dwelt in the country without break in the succession from generation to generation, and handed it down free to the present time by their valour. And if our more remote ancestors deserve praise, much more do our own fathers, who added to their inheritance the empire which we now possess, and spared no pains to be able to leave their acquisitions to us of the present generation. Lastly, there are few parts of our dominions that have not been augmented by those of us here, who are still more or less in the vigour of life; while the mother country has been furnished by us with everything that can enable her to depend on her own resources whether for war or for peace. That part of our history which tells of the military achievements which gave us our several possessions, or of the ready valour with which either we or our fathers stemmed the tide of Hellenic or foreign aggression, is a theme too familiar to my hearers for me to dilate on, and I shall therefore pass it by. But what was the road by which we reached our position, what the form of government under which our greatness grew, what the national habits out of which it sprang; these are questions which I may try to solve before I proceed to my panegyric upon these men; since I think this to be a subject upon which on the present occasion a speaker may properly dwell, and to which the whole assemblage, whether citizens or foreigners, may listen with advantage.*

> *Our constitution does not copy the laws of neighbouring states; we are rather a pattern to others than imitators ourselves. Its administration favours the many instead of the few; this is why it is called a democracy. If we look to the laws, they afford equal justice to all in their private differences; if no social standing, advancement in public life falls to reputation for capacity, class considerations not being allowed to interfere with merit; nor again does poverty bar the way, if a man is able to serve the state, he is not hindered by the obscurity of his condition. The freedom which we enjoy in our government extends also to our ordinary life. There, far from exercising a jealous surveillance over each other, we do not feel called upon to be angry with our neighbour for doing what he likes, or even to indulge in those injurious looks which cannot fail to be offensive, although they inflict no positive penalty. But all this ease in our private relations does not make us lawless as citizens. Against this fear is our chief safeguard, teaching us to obey the magistrates and the laws, particularly such as regard the protection of the injured, whether they are actually on the statute book, or belong to that code which, although unwritten, yet cannot be broken without acknowledged disgrace.*

## Weekly Connection

- The Persian Empire grew very large, from Greece all the way to India. When the Persians tried to conquer Greece, Athens and Sparta joined forces to fight them. The Greeks won important battles, including the battles of Marathon and Salamis, showing that they were stronger when united.

- After the Persian Wars, Athens led the Delian League, an alliance to keep Greece safe. Athens started using the league for its own power and money. This upset other city-states. Sparta and its allies created the Peloponnesian League to challenge Athens. The rivalry between Athens and Sparta led to the Peloponnesian War. Athens was strong on the sea, but Sparta was strong on land. In the end, Sparta won, but both sides were left weaker. This fighting made the Greek city-states less powerful.

- Pericles's funeral oration reminded Athenians of their democracy and achievements, even while they were at war. His speech showed what Athenians valued the most.
